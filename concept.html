The Idea

Serendipity, without
the surveillance.

The best moments with people are often unplanned: you both happened to be in the same place at the same time. We are building the infrastructure for that, while treating your location as something you lend for a moment, not something you surrender forever.

"The map era told you where everyone is, always. We think the better question is simpler: is anyone I would actually want to see nearby right now?"

The problem with always-on location

Continuous location sharing made a quiet trade: convenience in exchange for a permanent record of your movements, held by someone else. To find a friend once, you broadcast yourself constantly. Most people respond the only sensible way, by leaving it off, and the serendipity disappears with it.

Proximity should not require surveillance. Knowing a friend is in the same cafe does not require anyone to keep a 24/7 history of where you both have been.

Our answer: consent is the on-switch

Discovery only happens when you turn it on, and you are only ever matched with people you deliberately chose. Turning discovery on is the consent. There is no awkward "accept this person" popup, and there is no way to be found by someone you did not pick.

The moment you turn it off, you are gone from the system. Nothing about your presence outlives your intent to be present.

Why it matters beyond the app

This is a model for social technology that does not run on continuous extraction. Real-world connection, brokered by trust you already have, with privacy as the default rather than a setting buried three menus deep.

We see Project Proximity as the first product built on that principle, with room to grow into campuses, events, communities, and anywhere people want to find each other in person without giving up their map.

For the people using it

Less coordination, more spontaneity. See who is genuinely around, set a vibe like "coffee free", and meet up in real life. You stay invisible to everyone you did not choose.

For those backing it

A privacy-native take on a category usually defined by data harvesting. Defensible engineering, a clear trust model, and a wedge into real-world social discovery that mapping apps structurally cannot offer.
